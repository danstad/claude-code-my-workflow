Methods

This paper combines two econometric approaches that are rarely used together: a structural procurement auction model to recover firm costs from observed bids, and a stochastic frontier analysis (SFA) to measure how efficiently each firm uses inputs. Step 1: Structural Procurement Auction Model

Setup

The government runs a first-price sealed-bid procurement auction. There are \(n\) risk-neutral bidders, of two types:

  • Type 1 (\(n_1\) bidders): firms that have at some point received a direct contract or I3P invitation
  • Type 0 (\(n_0\) bidders): firms that have only participated in public auctions, with \(n_0 + n_1 = n\)

Each firm \(i\) of type \(j \in \{0, 1\}\) has a private cost \(c_{ji}\) drawn independently from \(F_j(\cdot)\), defined on \([\underline{c}, \bar{c}]\). Costs are independent private values (IPV) — a natural assumption for small, homogeneous pavement projects where a common value component is unlikely.

Equilibrium and Identification

At Bayesian Nash Equilibrium, each bidder chooses a bid to maximize expected profit. For a Type 1 firm:

\[b_{1i} - c_{1i} = \frac{1}{(n_1 - 1)\,\frac{g_1(b_{1i})}{1 - G_1(b_{1i})} + n_0\,\frac{g_0(b_{1i})}{1 - G_0(b_{1i})}}\]

where \(G_j(\cdot)\) and \(g_j(\cdot)\) are the bid CDF and PDF of type \(j\) firms. A symmetric equation holds for Type 0. This is the GPV inversion formula (Guerre, Perrigne & Vuong 2000), extended to asymmetric bidders by Flambard & Perrigne (2006).

NoteThe key insight

The equilibrium condition directly maps observed bids to unobserved costs — without needing to know the equilibrium strategies explicitly. The bid distributions \(G_0(\cdot)\) and \(G_1(\cdot)\) are estimated nonparametrically from the data.

Estimation

The estimation proceeds in three nonparametric steps:

  1. Estimate bid distributions \(\hat{G}_j(\cdot)\) and \(\hat{g}_j(\cdot)\) using kernel density estimators, conditioning on project characteristics \(\mathbf{z}\) (concrete volume, sewage dummy)
  2. Recover pseudo-costs \(\hat{c}_{ji}\) by plugging estimated bid densities into the inversion formula above
  3. Estimate cost distributions \(\hat{f}_j(\cdot)\) from the recovered pseudo-costs

Practical details: a triweight kernel is used for continuous variables; a smoothed binary kernel for the sewage dummy; 10% boundary trimming is applied; and bids are log-transformed to reduce skewness.

Step 2: Stochastic Frontier Analysis

The Cost Frontier

Given a level of output \(z\) (cubic meters of concrete) and input prices \(w\) (concrete price, construction wages, machinery rental), each firm minimizes cost subject to a production constraint. Allowing for technical inefficiency \(\eta \geq 0\), the firm’s actual cost relates to the optimal (frontier) cost by:

\[C^a = C^*(w, z) \cdot e^\eta\]

where \(\eta\) is the excess cost — the proportional amount by which the firm overuses all inputs relative to the efficient frontier. A firm with \(\eta = 0\) is fully efficient; \(\eta > 0\) means it could produce the same output with fewer inputs.

The SFA Model

Taking logs and adding a noise term \(\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\varepsilon^2)\):

\[\ln C^a = \ln C^*(w, z) + \eta + \varepsilon\]

The inefficiency term \(\eta \geq 0\) is estimated under two distributional assumptions (for robustness):

Model Distribution of \(\eta\)
Half-normal \(\eta \sim \lvert \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_u^2) \rvert\)
Truncated-normal \(\eta \sim \mathcal{N}^+(\mu, \sigma_u^2)\)

The efficiency index reported in Results is \(BC = e^{-\eta}\), bounded between 0 and 1. A value close to 1 means the firm is near the cost frontier (low excess cost); values further from 1 indicate greater inefficiency.

Connection Between Steps 1 and 2

The pseudo-costs \(\hat{c}_{ji}\) from Step 1 serve as the dependent variable \(C^a\) in the SFA. This is the methodological novelty of the paper: auction methods provide the cost data that SFA requires but typically cannot observe directly.

By comparing the efficiency index distributions of Type 1 and Type 0 firms, we can test whether the government’s discretionary selection of firms corresponds to genuine cost advantages.
