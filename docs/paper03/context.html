Context & Data

Mexico’s Procurement System

Mexico’s Law of Public Works and Related Services requires governments to allocate infrastructure contracts through the procedure that minimizes construction costs. In practice, this means three possible mechanisms:

Mechanism 1
Public Auction

Open to all qualified firms. First-price sealed-bid. The law favors this procedure. 617 contracts in 2011–2018.

Mechanism 2
Invitation (I3P)

Government invites ≥ 3 firms. Used when the project is small or urgent. 2,633 contracts in 2011–2018.

Mechanism 3
Direct Allocation

Government selects one firm without competition. Allowed under specific legal exceptions. 448 contracts in 2011–2018.

The exceptions allowing governments to bypass public auctions are deliberately broad — including vague language about “significant losses” or “force majeure” — giving local officials wide discretion in practice.

The Two Types of Firms

The key distinction in this study is between firms based on their history with the government:

  • Type 1 — “Government-selected”: firms that have received at least one contract by direct allocation or been invited to an I3P auction. More experienced, more politically connected.
  • Type 0 — “Public-auction-only”: firms that have only ever competed in open public auctions. Less experience, no special relationship with the government.

The central question: are Type 1 firms actually more cost-efficient, or are they just better connected?

Note on project size: The average project paves 8–9 blocks (50 m each), using hydraulic concrete — a relatively homogeneous, small-scale construction task. This homogeneity is intentional: it reduces unobserved heterogeneity in the structural model.

Evidence of Asymmetry Between Firm Types

Before structural estimation, simple reduced-form evidence already reveals important differences between firm types:

Bidding behavior: Type 1 firms bid on average 8.5% higher than Type 0 firms in the same auction, even after controlling for project characteristics. This is consistent with Type 1 firms having lower costs but shading bids upward, or alternatively, being selected for non-cost reasons.

Experience gap: Type 1 firms competed in an average of 6.6 contracts (all mechanisms) versus only 3.4 for Type 0 firms. In public auctions alone, Type 1 firms averaged 4.5 auctions vs. 3.4 for Type 0 firms.

Bid distributions: A Kolmogorov-Smirnov test rejects equality of bid distributions at the 25th and 75th percentiles of project size.

NoteHow are pseudo-costs estimated?

The observed bids are not firms’ costs — they include a strategic markup. The structural auction model (explained in Methods) recovers each firm’s underlying cost from its bid, using the equilibrium relationship between bidding strategies and cost distributions. These recovered values are called pseudo-costs.

Choice of Allocation Mechanism

A probit model on the choice of mechanism (public auction = 0, direct/I3P = 1) reveals that:

  • Project size: A one-standard-deviation increase in concrete volume reduces the probability of bypassing a public auction by 11.4 percentage points. Smaller projects are more likely to use discretionary mechanisms.
  • Sewage work: Projects with sewage work are 9.8 pp less likely to be directly allocated or invited — complex projects go to public auction more often.
  • Election year: Being in the year before municipal elections increases the probability of bypassing a public auction by 6.2 pp.
  • Party (PAN vs. PRI): PAN-governed municipalities are 18.9 pp less likely to use discretionary mechanisms than PRI municipalities.
