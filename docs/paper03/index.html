Productivity in Procurement Auctions of Pavement Contracts in Mexico

Productivity in Procurement Auctions of Pavement Contracts in Mexico

Daniel Prudencio  ·  Tecnológico de Monterrey  ·  Journal of Productivity Analysis, 2023

The Question

When local governments allocate public infrastructure contracts, they can choose between open competition (public auctions) or greater hiring discretion — inviting a handful of firms or directly picking a preferred contractor.

In theory, discretion allows governments to select the most capable firms. In practice, it opens the door to favoritism and corruption. Which is actually happening in Mexico?

This paper answers that question using street pavement contracts in Mexico (2011–2018), combining a structural auction model with stochastic frontier analysis to recover firm-level productivity and compare it across allocation mechanisms.

Key Findings

Complex Contracts
Government selects the right firms ✓

For projects that include sewage work, firms selected to settings with less competition have lower costs than firms that only participate in public auctions. Economies of scope likely explain this advantage.

Simple Contracts
Government selects the wrong firms ✗

For small, simple pavement projects (76% of invitation-only auctions), selected firms have higher costs. These contracts should be put out to public auction.

Political Factors
Electoral cycle drives discretion

Governments are 6.2% more likely to bypass public auctions in the year before elections. Firm excess costs rise 13.8% near election time in public auctions.

Abstract

When allocating contracts, governments decide between exercising hiring discretion or allowing higher competition without firm selection. Ex-ante, it is not clear which allocation format leads to better outcomes. The trade-off depends in part on the government’s ability to select the best firms when restricting competition and on the probability that this practice leads to corruption. In this paper, I study the allocation of street pavement contracts in Mexico. I combine auction methods with a productivity analysis to indirectly test whether local governments select firms with low excess costs when restricting competition. This indirect approach allows the monitoring of contract allocation in settings with little information on firm reputation. I find that firms selected to settings with less competition have lower costs in complex pavement contracts, but higher costs in simple ones. Contrary to the current practice, the results suggest that the government would benefit from using public auctions for simple pavement contracts.
