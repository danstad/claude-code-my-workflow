The headline result: The pandemic increased discretionary contracting by 17% on average, but this effect is entirely driven by non-healthcare institutions. For healthcare institutions the estimate is negative and statistically insignificant. The non-healthcare coefficient (+0.166) represents a 23% increase relative to the pre-pandemic baseline of 0.715.

Dynamic Effects: Event Study

The event study plot above (Figure 1 in the paper) shows the monthly path of the DCVB ratio before and after the lockdown. Three patterns stand out:

1. Parallel trends hold pre-pandemic. For all three panels, the pre-pandemic coefficients (\(q = -9\) through \(q = -2\)) cluster near zero and are statistically insignificant. This supports the identifying assumption.

2. The effect peaks and fades. For overall and non-health DCVB, the sharpest increase occurs at \(q = 2\) (May 2020), the month the stay-at-home order was lifted. The effect persists for approximately four months before returning to pre-pandemic levels, consistent with the resilience of crime theory (Frailing & Harper, 2017) rather than theories that predict a permanent increase.

3. Healthcare is flat throughout. The health DCVB panel shows no meaningful increase at any point during the pandemic. The coefficients are small and statistically indistinguishable from zero post-lockdown.

Robustness

The paper implements four robustness checks:

Note1. Multiple hypothesis testing (FDR q-values)

Because three outcome columns are tested simultaneously (overall, health, non-health), there is a risk of false discovery. The paper applies the False Discovery Rate correction of Anderson (2008), replacing p-values with q-values. The main results survive.

Note2. Oster (2019) bounds for omitted variable bias

This methodology simulates a bound around the parameter of interest based on an expected value of the R². When the bounds exclude zero, it indicates that the parameter of interest remains robust to omitted variable bias. The Oster bounds exclude zero for the DiD results on overall DCVB and non-healthcare DCVB, confirming that those results are not sensitive to the problem of omitted variables. No significant result is found for healthcare DCVB.

Note3. Outlier exclusion

Results are reproduced after removing the top 1% and top 5% of contracts by value. The coefficients remain stable and statistically significant for overall and non-health DCVB.

Note4. Excluding SEDENA (the Mexican Army)

The Mexican Army expanded its role across government sectors during the pandemic. The paper checks whether the non-health result is driven by SEDENA alone. Excluding SEDENA does not change the conclusion.

Mechanisms

Beyond the main DiD result, the paper investigates two potential mechanisms linking the pandemic to higher non-healthcare DCVB.

Market Concentration

A Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) is constructed for each institution-month, measuring how concentrated the supplier market is for each institution:

\[\text{HHI}_{iyt} = \sum_{j=1}^{n_{iyt}} s_{jiyt}^2\]

where \(s_{jiyt}\) is the share of contracts given to provider \(j\) in institution \(i\), year \(y\), month \(t\).

The pandemic increased HHI for non-healthcare institutions by approximately 462 points (statistically significant at 10%). The pre-pandemic baseline HHI for non-health institutions was 2,819, already above the U.S. Department of Justice threshold of 2,500 for highly concentrated markets. A 462-point increase in an already concentrated market represents a meaningful further reduction in competition.

Healthcare institutions show no significant change in market concentration.

Late Disclosure of Contract Information

The paper also tests whether the pandemic changed the proportion of contracts for which procedural information was published after the contract’s starting date (a proxy for opacity). For non-healthcare institutions, the proportion of late-disclosed contracts increased by approximately 10% (coefficient 0.062, significant at 5%). No significant change is found for healthcare institutions.

Putting the mechanisms together: Non-healthcare institutions simultaneously became more discretionary (higher DCVB), more concentrated in their supplier base (higher HHI), and less transparent in their disclosure practices (more late publications). These three patterns are consistent with an increase in corruption risk. The effect is not driven by any single institution, as the leave-one-out robustness check confirms.

Silverio-Murillo, Prudencio & Balmori-de-la-Miyar — Public Organization Review (2024)
